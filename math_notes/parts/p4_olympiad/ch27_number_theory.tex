\subsubsection*{Calendar Arithmetic: Tracking Weekdays with Modular Arithmetic}

A practical application of $\pmod{7}$ arithmetic is computing the day of the week for any date.

\begin{remark}[Day-of-week shifts]
The day of the 1st of month $m$ shifts forward by the number of days in the previous month, taken $\pmod{7}$:
\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{ll}
31-day month & $31 \equiv 3 \pmod{7}$ \\
30-day month & $30 \equiv 2 \pmod{7}$ \\
28-day February & $28 \equiv 0 \pmod{7}$ \\
29-day February (leap) & $29 \equiv 1 \pmod{7}$
\end{tabular}
\end{center}
So a standard year shifts the weekday forward by $365 \equiv 1 \pmod 7$, and a leap year by $366 \equiv 2 \pmod 7$.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[Leap year rule]
A year $y$ is a leap year iff: $4 \mid y$, \emph{except} century years require $400 \mid y$. Thus 1900 is \emph{not} a leap year, but 2000 \emph{is}.
\end{remark}

\begin{example}[Sundays on the 1st, 1901--2000]
Given: 1 Jan 1900 was a Monday. Since 1900 is not a leap year, $365 \equiv 1 \pmod 7$, so 1 Jan 1901 was a Tuesday.

Starting from Tuesday, iterate through each month from Jan 1901 to Dec 2000, shifting the weekday by the appropriate amount $\pmod 7$ at each step, and count how many 1st-of-month dates land on Sunday (weekday $= 0$ if we label Mon $= 1$, \ldots, Sun $= 0$).

Over the 1200 months in $[1901, 2000]$ there are 25 leap years: $1904, 1908, \ldots, 1996, 2000$.

Carrying through the arithmetic gives: \textbf{171 Sundays}.
\end{example}

\begin{remark}[Encoding days as integers]
Label days $0, 1, \ldots, 6$ (e.g.\ Mon $= 0$, \ldots, Sun $= 6$). Each month transition adds the previous month's length $\pmod 7$. A programmatic solution loops over all 1200 months and increments a running counter.
\end{remark}
